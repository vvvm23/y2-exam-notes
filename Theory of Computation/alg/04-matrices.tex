\subsection{Simple Divide-and-Conquer}

Simply partition the matrices into multiple parts and solve each independently:

\begin{center}
\begin{math}

    C = A \cdot B = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        A_{1,1} & A_{1,2} \\
        A_{2,1} & A_{2,2}
    \end{bmatrix}
    \cdot
    \begin{bmatrix}
        B_{1,1} & B_{1,2} \\
        B_{2,1} & B_{2,2}
    \end{bmatrix}
    =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        C_{1,1} & C_{1,2} \\
        C_{2,1} & C_{2,2}
    \end{bmatrix}

\end{math}
\end{center}

Where $X_{i,j}$ is a partition of matrix $X$ 

By Master Theorem, this naive algorithm has running time $\Theta(n^3)$ 

\subsection{Strassen's Algorithm}

A more sophisticated divide and conquer approach:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Divide Matrices into parts as before
    \item Create 10 matrices $S_1, ..., S_{10} \in \mathbb{R}^{n/2 \times n/2}$ obtained by sums or differences of the partitions of $A$ and $B$ .
    \item Recursively compute 7 product matrices $P_1, ..., P_7 \in \mathbb{R}^{n/2 \times n/2}$ 
    \item Compute partitions of $C$ by adding and subtracting combinations of $P$ 
\end{itemize}

Using Master Theorem, the worst-case running time is $\Theta(n^{log_27}) \approx \Theta(n^{2.8074})$
